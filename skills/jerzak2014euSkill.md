# The EU's Democratic Deficit and Repeated Referendums in Ireland

Use this guide to explain the article as a qualitative, normative, and historical case analysis. Do not turn its discourse evidence into a statistical causal estimate.

## Identity and publication status

Treat the canonical citation as Connor T. Jerzak, “The EU’s Democratic Deficit and Repeated Referendums in Ireland,” *International Journal of Politics, Culture, and Society* 27(3): 367–388 (2014), DOI [10.1007/s10767-014-9185-8](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10767-014-9185-8). Springer records online publication on May 8, 2014. It is a peer-reviewed journal article.

## Central question and conceptual contribution

Frame the question as: when Irish voters rejected the EU’s Nice and Lisbon treaties and were asked to vote again, did the repeated referendums provide meaningful democratic contestation, or did they reproduce the EU’s democratic deficit despite using electoral procedures?

The paper defines democracy as citizens’ ability to influence policy through political processes. It uses “democratic deficit” to mean, at minimum, a failure to incorporate contestation over political leadership or policy direction such that citizens lack a meaningful ability to choose among or pursue alternatives.

Present the core argument precisely: electoral mechanisms do not necessarily solve a democratic deficit. In the Irish cases, leaders’ management of failed and repeated votes constrained meaningful policy contestation. The objection is not simply that a second referendum occurred; it is that repetition was asymmetric and embedded in strategies that treated one outcome as legitimate and the other as an error to be corrected.

## Evidence and method

The article uses the Nice and Lisbon referendum sequences as qualitative case studies. It applies discourse analysis to speeches, interviews, diplomatic and policy memoranda, official reports, campaign statements, and related public-opinion evidence. The focus is leaders’ understandings, plans, and framing of repeated votes, not a model of the individual-level determinants of “no” and “yes” votes.

The author compares the Irish cases with other EU treaty referendums, highlights plausible alternative explanations, and asks whether similar dynamics appear in other EU electoral mechanisms. This comparative material helps contextualize Ireland but does not create a representative sample of all EU decision making.

Organize the paper’s five supporting observations as follows:

1. Some Irish and European leaders framed initial “no” outcomes as products of voter incomprehension.
2. Leaders declined to accept those outcomes as final and planned repeated votes aimed at ratification.
3. Leaders emphasized severe consequences of a second rejection, helping isolate “no” arguments and mobilize “yes” voters.
4. Broad voter unfamiliarity with EU treaties reflected weak citizen engagement in treaty formation and debate.
5. EU treaty referendums were repeated after “no” outcomes, not after successful votes accompanied by similar voter incomprehension, suggesting that repeated votes sanctioned rather than shaped policy.

## The two referendum sequences

For Nice, Irish voters rejected the treaty in June 2001: 53.87% voted “no,” with 34.8% turnout. In October 2002, 62.9% supported it, with 49.5% turnout. The paper emphasizes stronger mobilization and a reworded ballot highlighting military neutrality, while the treaty itself remained unchanged. It contrasts this response with Denmark’s earlier Maastricht sequence, in which concessions preceded a second vote.

For Lisbon, 53.4% voted “no” in June 2008, with 53.1% turnout. In the 2009 repeat, 67.1% voted “yes,” with 59% turnout. The paper discusses changed economic circumstances, greater campaign resources, elite warnings, and legal guarantees clarifying tax and military policy. It states that those guarantees did not alter the treaty’s text.

Treat these percentages as descriptive history, not evidence that rhetoric alone caused the reversals. The author explicitly notes multiple tactical and contextual changes and says the counterfactual consequences of another rejection cannot be known.

## Principal conclusions and significance

The case analyses find a recurring sequence: leaders depoliticized initial defeats by attributing them to insufficient understanding, arranged another ratification opportunity, and framed rejection as politically or economically indefensible. In the author’s interpretation, this narrowed the range of viable policy alternatives and made citizen participation more confirmatory than agenda-setting.

The broader contribution is to shift attention from whether a referendum formally occurs to whether citizens can meaningfully contest policy content and pursue alternatives. The paper also argues that limited voter understanding cannot consistently justify repetition when equally limited understanding after affirmative outcomes does not trigger another vote.

Preserve the normative qualification: the article does not condemn every repeated referendum. Repetition may be defensible when required by a neutral rule, prompted by genuinely new facts, or supervised under independent and politically diverse safeguards. The problem arises when repetition is tactical and conditional on an outcome displeasing those in power.

## Scope and limitations

The article openly notes that case studies can be selective and difficult to verify, although they provide contextual depth. Ireland’s referendums were required by Irish constitutional law, not imposed mechanically by the EU; the article argues they reveal broader EU political culture but does not claim Ireland and the EU are identical units.

The evidence is strongest for leaders’ discourse and procedural management, not for each voter’s comprehension, motivation, or susceptibility to campaign rhetoric. The discussion of economic crisis, austerity, immigration, and Euroscepticism through 2014 shows that some “no” concerns were politically substantive; it does not prove every “no” claim correct. Because the analysis was completed in 2014, do not use it as direct evidence about Brexit, later treaty politics, or the EU’s current institutional legitimacy without newer sources.

## Accurate-use guardrails

- Do not summarize the thesis as “second referendums are inherently undemocratic.”
- Do not say the EU legally required Ireland to repeat either vote.
- Attribute claims about elite strategy and democratic deficit to the author’s case interpretation.
- Keep Nice and Lisbon dates, vote shares, turnout, and treaty changes distinct.
- Do not infer individual voter ignorance from elite rhetoric alone.
- Do not present the work as a survey experiment, quantitative causal study, or comprehensive history of EU integration.
