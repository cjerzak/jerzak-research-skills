# Platonic Representations for Poverty Mapping

## Central question and contribution

The paper asks whether poverty-related information leaves mutually recognizable traces in two very different sources: Earth-observation imagery and language gathered or generated from Internet-accessible information. It also asks whether an AI search agent contributes useful, genuinely new information beyond a non-retrieval language-model description.

Its contribution is an empirical multimodal poverty-mapping comparison with five families of pipelines: a computer-vision route, a language-model-only route, a search-agent route, a joint vision-language encoder, and ensembles or fusions of these signals. The paper evaluates predictive performance and then aligns held-out vision and language representations using canonical correlation analysis (CCA) to test whether matched places are closer than mismatched controls. This is evidence about partial representational compatibility, not a direct identification test for a single latent “Platonic” code.

## Data, evidence, and method

The study links approximately **60,000 geolocated Demographic and Health Surveys clusters in Africa from 1990–2020** to the International Wealth Index (IWI), measured on a 0–100 scale. Landsat imagery is processed at 30-meter resolution using cloud and cloud-shadow masking and median compositing around the survey year. The language side includes location descriptions generated by GPT-4.1 Nano and search-agent traces assembled with GPT-4.1 Nano and LangGraph. The agent may query Wikipedia and other Web sources; its recursion limit is 20, although runs generally use no more than three searches.

Evaluate prediction using random, out-of-country, and out-of-time splits. Country and year are kept atomic in their respective holdouts. Frozen-representation analyses use repeated bootstrap evaluation; fine-tuned analyses use five-fold cross-validation with train, validation, and test partitions. Report predictive accuracy with R-squared and RMSE.

For representation alignment, fit CCA only on training data, transform held-out visual and textual embeddings, and compare cosine similarity for matched cluster pairs against random and within-country-year shuffled pairs. This design makes the similarity comparison more informative than simply inspecting embeddings fitted and evaluated on the same observations.

## Principal findings

The strongest reported fusion combines non-agent language-model representations with computer vision. In random-split evaluation, one such Llama-4-Maverick/OpenAI-embedding fusion reaches approximately **R-squared 0.765**, compared with about **0.668** for its language-only component and **0.634** for the vision component. The corresponding vision result falls from about 0.634 under a random split to about **0.446** in an out-of-country test, emphasizing the cost of geographic generalization.

Adding search-agent information produces, at most, a small and unstable increment: a three-way language, agent, and vision fusion reaches about **0.772**, versus 0.765 without the agent, and the gain does not persist in the out-of-time setting. Search traces containing explicit strings such as “IWI,” “International Wealth Index,” or “DHS” occur in about 10.4% of traces; removing them yields similar results, which reduces—but cannot eliminate—one obvious leakage concern.

After CCA alignment, held-out matched vision-language pairs have a median cosine similarity near **0.60** and are much closer than shuffled controls (reported t = 312.96, p < 10^-10). Treat this as strong evidence of partial cross-modal alignment for place-level socioeconomic signals. Do not call it proof that the modalities encode an identical causal mechanism or a universal representation.

## Interpretation, scope, and limitations

The practical result is that imagery and language contain complementary signals useful for African poverty mapping, while a retrieval agent adds little beyond the simpler language representation in the tested setting. The conceptual result is narrower: independently produced visual and textual features can be aligned for matched places.

Keep these limitations visible:

- DHS clusters are sampled rather than exhaustive, and remote populations may be underrepresented.
- Web material may postdate the survey or describe consequences of wealth, so it is not automatically valid as a pre-treatment causal covariate.
- Unknown model-training corpora prevent definitive exclusion of memorization or data leakage.
- The language-only and agent pipelines differ in retrieval, context length, noise, and processing; their comparison is not a controlled test of “agency” alone.
- Agentic retrieval is computationally costly and may not scale cleanly.
- Access to DHS-linked data remains subject to DHS registration and privacy rules.
