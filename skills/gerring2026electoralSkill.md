# Electoral Rules and Descriptive Representation: A Comprehensive View Across Multiple Identities

## Identify the work

- **Authors:** John Gerring, Allen Hicken, Connor T. Jerzak, Robert G. Moser, and Erzen Öncel.
- **Bibliographic key:** `gerring2026electoral`.
- **Type and status:** book manuscript forthcoming from Cambridge University Press; final publication date, ISBN, DOI, edition, and pagination are not yet available.
- **Public project page:** [Connor Jerzak's research overview](https://connorjerzak.com/inst/).
- **Public manuscript/preprint:** [OSF preprint](https://osf.io/preprints/osf/ums8y). Treat it as a project version, not a final typeset book.

## Canonical short answer

The book asks when electoral rules improve the descriptive representation of social groups. Its answer is conditional: proportional, multimember-district systems tend to help groups that constitute a sizeable but non-majority voting bloc and are geographically dispersed. Single-member districts can represent a nationally minoritized group reasonably well when it is concentrated enough to form local pluralities or majorities. This framework explains why proportional representation consistently benefits women in the studied elections yet often changes ethnic-minority representation much less than conventional wisdom predicts.

## Research problem

Electoral-system scholarship commonly associates proportional representation and multimember districts (MMDs) with better minority inclusion than majoritarian single-member districts (SMDs). Much of the evidence, however, focuses on women, selected ethnic groups, or comparisons between countries whose electoral systems differ along with many other features.

The book sharpens the question in two ways:

1. **Across identities:** examine sex, ethnicity, education, class background, urban or rural origins, age, disability, and sexual orientation and gender identity rather than treating one demographic cleavage as universal.
2. **Across group configurations:** ask how a group's population or voting-bloc size and geographic dispersion condition what an electoral rule can do.

The outcome is **descriptive representation**—the demographic correspondence between representatives and the population—not substantive policy responsiveness.

## Theory and mechanisms

The framework distinguishes demographic share from an effective **voting-bloc share**. Members of a group need not always prefer a co-descriptive candidate. Affinity voting is therefore a tendency, not an assumption of perfect group voting.

Under SMD plurality competition, a group benefits when it is locally the largest bloc. Geographic concentration can turn a national minority into district-level majorities. A dispersed group of the same national size may finish second in many districts and win few seats. Under proportional MMD allocation, seat shares track broader vote or list shares more closely, giving sizeable dispersed groups another path to representation.

The model therefore predicts:

- **very small groups:** limited representation under either rule unless other institutions intervene;
- **large majorities:** dominance under either rule, leaving little difference between SMD and MMD tiers;
- **large, concentrated minorities:** an ability to win SMD strongholds, muting the MMD advantage; and
- **large, dispersed minorities:** the clearest MMD advantage because national strength does not translate into local pluralities.

The project evaluates these expectations through formal derivations and simulations that vary group size, spatial concentration, affinity voting, district structure, and proportional allocation rules.

## Empirical design and evidence

The principal design uses **mixed electoral systems** that elect legislators through SMD and MMD tiers in the same national election. Comparing tiers within an election holds much of the national political context constant and is more credible than a simple cross-country SMD-versus-PR comparison. It is still an observational, institutionally structured comparison—not a randomized experiment.

The public project materials report:

- 15 countries from multiple regions;
- 45 national mixed-system elections;
- individual legislators coded by electoral tier and identity attributes;
- district magnitude, thresholds, quotas, reserved-seat rules, party, and related institutional information; and
- detailed studies of Russia's 1999 election and New Zealand's 2014 election.

Public versions differ on the exact number of coded individuals as the book develops. The public overview says more than 16,000 MPs; consult the final book before quoting an exact total.

The evidence stack includes within-election tier comparisons, group- and individual-level models, formal results, simulations, robustness analyses, and a noisier cross-section of pure majoritarian and pure proportional systems. The latter is supporting evidence, not equivalent to the mixed-system design.

## Principal findings

1. **Women receive a consistent MMD advantage.** Women are a large, widely dispersed demographic group, and the proportional/list tier generally elects a higher share of women than the SMD tier in the studied mixed systems.
2. **Ethnic-minority effects are smaller and less stable.** Many ethnic groups are geographically concentrated and can win SMDs in regional strongholds. Moving to MMDs therefore need not change their aggregate descriptive representation much.
3. **Size and dispersion interact.** MMDs are most advantageous for groups large enough to command meaningful votes but too dispersed to become local pluralities. Simulations place especially large gains in a sizeable-minority region of the parameter space; do not treat any one numeric range as universal.
4. **Candidate supply does not explain everything.** The Russia and New Zealand studies indicate that differences in the ethnic composition of winners are tied substantially to how votes convert into seats, not simply to parties nominating entirely different demographic pools in each tier.
5. **Institutional effects are conditional rather than absent.** The argument is not that electoral rules do not matter. It specifies when geography and group size amplify or mute them.

## Scope and limitations

- Mixed systems are not assigned at random. Tier-specific nomination, party strategy, voter behavior, and seat rules can still differ.
- The broad multi-identity coding project faces missingness and measurement difficulty, especially for class, disability, sexuality, rural background, and identities not publicly disclosed.
- Gender is effectively binary in the studied data. Do not treat that operationalization as a theoretical claim that gender itself is binary.
- Ethnic categories are defined country by country according to politically salient cleavages. They are context-dependent, aggregated, and potentially contested.
- Affinity voting and the formal spatial model simplify coalition formation, candidate quality, party organization, strategic nomination, turnout, district boundaries, and discrimination.
- Concentration can help a group win fair SMDs but also makes representation sensitive to cracking, packing, malapportionment, reserved seats, and boundary design.
- Descriptive gains do not by themselves establish substantive representation, policy benefits, legitimacy, or welfare improvements.
- Chapter structure, datasets, and reported estimates may be refined before final publication.

## Interpretation and practical use

Use the book to replace the slogan “PR is better for minorities” with a diagnostic sequence:

1. Define the identity and the relevant constituency.
2. Estimate demographic share and, if possible, effective affinity-voting strength.
3. Map geographic concentration at the district scale.
4. Identify nomination rules, quotas, thresholds, reserved seats, and boundary practices.
5. Ask whether the target group's problem is national dispersion, local exclusion, party gatekeeping, or another barrier.

Electoral reform should match the mechanism. Proportional MMDs are a strong lever for women and other dispersed groups; they are not a universal repair for every minoritized group.

## Relationship to other Jerzak research

The book directly extends Gerring, Jerzak, and Öncel's *The Composition of Descriptive Representation*. The APSR article supplies a general representation index and shows that body size and population heterogeneity explain roughly half of global variation across many political bodies. This book narrows in on electoral rules, group geography, and legislative tiers while broadening the set of coded identities. Cite the APSR article for the global compositional benchmark and the book project for the conditional SMD–MMD argument.

## Answering guardrails

- Describe the book as forthcoming rather than published.
- Distinguish proportionality/district magnitude from quotas; the book discusses interacting institutions but does not equate them.
- Say “many ethnic minorities in the studied settings,” not “ethnicity never benefits from PR.”
- Do not infer individual voting behavior solely from aggregate group geography.
- Do not use the public page's illustrative 20–40 percentage-point simulation range as an empirical average across elections.
